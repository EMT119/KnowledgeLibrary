Chapter 22 - Immunologic Emergencies

Anatomy and Physiology Review

The immune system protects the body from substances and organisms that are foreign to the body. There are two types of systems, cellular immunity and humoral immunity. Cellular immunity, this is where the body creates specialized white blood cells called T-cells that attack and destory invaders. Humoral immunity is when B cell lymphocytes produce anitbodies that dissolve in the plasma and lymph to wage ware on invading organisms. These cells are located throughout the body in the lymph nodes, spleen, and gastrointestinal tract.

Under the right circumstances almost any substance can cause an allergic reaction. Although allergens can come from anything they will usually fall into one of five categories.

  • Insect bites and stings
    • This occurs when an insect bites or stings you and injects venom into your body. Injecting venom is called envenomation
  • Medications
    • Depending on the route of medication it could be immediate and severe (injected medications) or take up to 30 minutes (oral). Just because someone has taken a medication before does not mean they cannot have a reaction to that medication.
  • Plants
    • This can be caused by dust, pollen, mold, mildew, and other organic materials
  • Foods
    • This can be caused by ingesting food that someone is allergic to. These reactions take more than 30 minutes to appear, however the reaction can be quite severe.
  • Chemicals
    • Certain chemicals can cause a reaction. This could be makeup, soap, hair dye, latex, or other substances.

An antibody is a protein the body produces in response to a antigen.

Physiology Review

The first encounter with a foreign material begins the primary response which mainly involves the white blood cells. When these immune cells engulf the invaders they will record one or two proteins on the surface of the substance to help design antibodies to match and inactivate each substance.

Basophils are stationed in specific sties within the tissues. Mast cells are on patrol. These cells produce "chemical weapons" also known as chemical mediators. The way this works if these substances are encapsulated and only released if an antigen invades and combines with one of the antibodies. If this happens the granules are ejected from mast cells. These chemical mediators summon white blood cells to the area. This also increases the blood flow to the area by dilating the blood vessels and increasing capillary permeability.

There are two types of immunitys, acquired and natural. acquired immunity is produced by administering a vaccine that allows the body to produce anitbodies with experiencing the disease. Natural immunity is when the body encounters the antigen and experiences a full immune response.

Allergic Reactions Versus Anaphylaxis

There are two types of reactions. Local reactions is when the response is localized to a specific area, for example an insect bite will swell at the site where the bite occured. Systemic reactions occur throughout the body and usually involve multiple systems, examples could include someone who has eaten something they are allergic to. Hypersensitivity is when the body responds in an exaggerated way after coming into contact with a substance.

Pathophysiologic Response

Someone with allergies are more sensitive than someone without. As someone with allergies may identify harmless substances as potential threats.

When the body reacts it releases two chemical mediators, histamine and leukotrienes. Histamine will cause blood vessels in a local area to dilate. Luekotrienes are also released but are more powerful that histamines, they also cause dilation and capillary leaking.

Emergency Medical Care

It is important for the provider to be able to distinguish between anaphylaxis versus other conditions. If you determine that your patient is in anaphylaxis follow the list below:

  • Remove the offending agent
  • Position the patient as appropriate
  • Apply high-flow oxygen, or assist ventilation as needed
  • Obtain IV access
  • Give fluid resuscitation
  • Administer epinephrine and an antihistamine (diphenhydramine for example) as indicated
  • Transport promptly
  • Frequently reassess the patient, and provide psychological support
