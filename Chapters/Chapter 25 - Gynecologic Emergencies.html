Anatomy and Physiology Review

The female reproductive organs include genitalia, uterus, vagina, fallopian tubes, ovaries, and perineum.

Ovaries are almond-shaped organs that are on each side of the upper pelvic cavity. Ovaries serve two functions. They help produce ova and estrogen and progesterone. The corpus luteum (small yellow endocrine structre that develops within a rupture ovarian follicle) screte the hormones.

Fallopian tubes are ducts that extend from the uterus and terminate near the ovary on each side.

Physiology Review

Every month hormones rise and stimulate developed of eggs in the ovaries, which causes enometrium of the urerus to thicken in anticipation of implantation of a fertilized egg. If implantation occurs menses is suspended until the pregnancy ends. If implantation does NOT occur then menstruation begins. Menstruation is the shedding of the uterine lining that occurs roughly every 28 days. These cycles usually last 4 to 6 days with blood loss around 25 to 60 mL. The inital onset of menstruation is known as menarche which occurs during puberty. Menopause is the cessation of menstruation and ovarian function, generally occuring between the ages of 45 and 55 years.

Premenstrual syndrome (PMS) is a group of symptoms that occurs 7 to 14 days before the onset of menstrual flow and will typically subside once the flow begins. PMS will affect 20 to 30% of premenopausal women, these symptoms can be debilitating. Certain things can exacerbate these symptoms such as: stress, diet, alcohol consumption, prescription and nonprescription drug use. The main form of treatment for these patients is administering oral or IV glucose if glucose levels fall. As well as administering analgesics (painkillers) or anxiolytics (anti-anxiety)

In some cases women may experience abdominal pain or cramping 2 weeks before the beginning of menses. These pains and accompanying symptoms are collectively called mittelschmerz (german word for middle pain), this affects approximately 20% of women. This pain usually is not severe and lasts from a few minutes to 48 hours.

Amenorrhea is the absscence of cessation of menses. Although many factors can cause this the most common cause is pregnancy. It can also be caused by exercise, emotional problems, or extreme stress.

Pathyophysiology

Many disorders can lead to gynecologic emergencies. Some of these disorders include: acute or chronic infections, hemorrhage, rupture of a cyst, and rupture of an ectopic pregnancy.

Pelvic Inflammatory Disease

Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) is an acute or chronic infection of the upper reproductive organs. This is caused by organisms entering the vagina during sexual activity and migrating through the opening of the cervix and into the uterine cavity. Symptoms for this are usually as follows:

  • Sepsis
  • Abscess formation
  • Generalized peritonitis
  • Infertility

Sexually Transmitted Disease

Unfortunately there is not much you can do as an Advanced EMT for these patients except for placing the patient in position of comfort and providing supportive care.

Below are some of the diseases that you may run into.

Bacterial Vaginosis

This infection is most common in women ages 15 to 44. In these infections bacteria in the vagina is replaced by overgrowth of other bacterial forms. Symptoms can include: itching, burning, or pain. It may also be accompanied by a "fishy", foul-smelling discharge. Pregnancy complications can occur from this infection with premature babies or babies with low birth weight. If this infection is untreated it can lead to more serious infections or result in PID.

Chancroid

This disease is caused by the bacterium called Haemophilus dureyi. It is highly contagious but curable disease. This disease causes ulcers (painful sores) usually on the genitals. Swollen, painful lymph glands or inguinal buboes are usually present as well. Women can be asymptomatic and therefore unaware they have the disease.

Chlamydia

Caused by the bacterium called Chlamydia trachomatis. It is one of the most common STDs in the United States. Symptoms are usually mild or absent, some women may experience lower abdominal pain, low back pain, nausea, fever, pain during sexual intercourse, and/or bleeding between menstrual periods. If the infection spread to the rectum and lead to rectal pain, discharge, or bleeding. If untreated it can progress to PID. In some but rare cases it can cause arthritis, which is usually accompanied by skin lesions and inflammation of the eye and urethra also known as Reiter syndrome

Cytomegalovirus (CMV)

This disease is a member of the herpesvirus. It is a common viral infection but has no cure. Although it can lie dormant in the body for years, in its active stages CMV can produce the following symptoms: prolong high fever, chills, headache, malaise, extreme fatigue, and an enlarged spleen. Individuals with immune disorders, those receiving chemotherapy, and pregnant women can haver serious complications develop such as fever, pneumonia, liver infection, and anemia. Newborns who acquire CMV are susceptible to lung conditions, blood conditions, liver conditions, swollen glands, rash, and poor weight gain.

Genital Herpes

Infection cuased by the herpes simplex virus. This infection usually affects the genitals, buttocks, or anal area. There are two types

Type I

Most common form. Infects the mouth and lips which causes cold sores or "fever" blisters. It can also produce sores on the genitals

Type II

This form is a more serious infection. It can affect the mouth but is primarly known as the primary cause of genital herpes

In active herpes the symptoms will usually appear within 2 weeks of primary infeciton and can last for several weeks. Symptoms can include tingling or sores near the area where the virus entered the body (genital or rectal area, buttocks, thighs, or parts of the body where there is broken skin. In women the sores can present inside the vagina or on the cervic or urinary passageway.

Small red bumps will develop into blisters and become itchy, painful sores that can develop a crust and heal without leaving a scar. Other symptoms that can occur are fever, muscle aches and pains, headche, dysuria, vaginal discharge, and swollen glands in the groin area.

Gonorrhea

Cuased by the Neisseria gonorrhoeae bacterium. This bacterium grows and multiples rapidly in warm, moist areas in the reproductive tract. Some placeds that are good areas for this to occur are the cervic, uterus, and fallopian tubes in women, and urethra in both men and women.

Symptoms in men are generally severe. Symptoms start to appear 2 to 10 days after exposure. Women may be asymptomatic until the infection has spread to other parts of the reproductive system. However when symptoms do appear they are dysuria (painful urination), yellow or bloody vaginal discharge (usually with a foul odor).

Severe infections may present with cramping and abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, and bleeding between menstrual periods. When these symptoms appear it means the infection has progressed to PID. If the infection starts/moves to the rectum then anal discharge, itching, and painful bowel movements with fecal blood spotting will present. If the method of infection is through oral sex then it is called gonococcal pharyngitis. The symptoms associtated with this form of the infection are generally mild with symptoms presenting as painful or difficult swallowing, sore throat, swollen lymph glands, fever, headache, and nasal congestion. If this is not treated then it can enter the bloodstream and spread throughout the body, when it spreads to the brain it is known as disseminated gonococcemia

Genital Warts

Caused by the human papillomarvirus (HPV). This is the most common form of STDs, and it is estimated that 79 millon people are infected and roughly 14 million new cases will occur every year. Some of the infected individuals have no symptoms but other will generally will have growths develop in the vagina, vulva, cervix, or the penis and scrotum in men. HPV can be contributing cause of cancer. In pregnant women wart can develop that could impede urination or obstruct the birth canal. If this virus is passed to the fetus then laryngeal papillomatosis (throat warts that block the airway) may develop which is potentially life-threatening.

Syphilis

Caused by the bacterium treponema pallidum. This disease is known as the "great imitator" as it can be difficult to distinguish it from other diseases. Transmission can occur through direct contact with open sores which may be anywhere on the body but usually are located on the genitals, anus, rectum, lips, or mouth. It is important to remeber that a person can remain asymptomatic for years. The disease can be split into three stages.

Primary Stage

This is when a single sore otherwise known as a chancre develops. This sore is generally painless and is small, firm, and round. It will go away after 3 to 6 weeks, after this the disease will have progressed to the second stage.

Secondary Stage

This stage usually has mucous membrane lesions and rash develop. The rash does not generally itch. It is usaully accompanied by other symptoms such as: fever, swollen lymph glands, sore throat, patchy hair loss, headache, weight loss, muscle ache, and fatigue. Although the symptoms will resolve without treatment it will also allow the disease to progress to the last stage.

Tertiary Stage

At this point in the disease it will start to attack the brain, nerves, eyes, heart, blood vessels, liver, bones, and joints. It can take years for the damage to become evident. Some other things the disease can cause are paralysis, numbness, dementia, gradual blindness, and difficulty coordinating muscle moves. In pregnant women their babies can be stillborn, blind, developmentally delayed, or die shortly after birth

Trichomoniasis

This is caused by the protozoan parasite called Trichomonas vaginalis. The parasite is transmitted through sexual contact. Individuals may be asymptomatic or may have frothy, yellow-green vaginal discharge with a strong odor. During intercourse these individuals can expereience discomfort as well as irritation, itching, lower abdominal pain, or dysuria. Symptoms typically appear 5 to 28 days after exposure. This infection can cause low birth weight or premature babies in pregnancy, as well as the increased susceptibility to HIV infection.
