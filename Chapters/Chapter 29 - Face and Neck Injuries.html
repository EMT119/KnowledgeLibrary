Anatomy and Physiology Review

Bones and Landmarks

The head is divided into two parts: the cranium and the face. The cranium, or skull, contains the brain. The face is composed of the eyes, ears, nose, mouth, and cheeks. The facial skeleton is composed of six major bones: the nasal bone, the two maxillae (upper jaw bones), the two zygomas (cheek bones), and the mandible (jaw bone)

One bone not shown in the images above is the hyoid bone it functions to stablize and support the larynx and is a point of attachment for many neck and tongue muscles

The Eye

The eye is approximately 1 inch in diameter. It is located inside the orbit and is held in by loose connective tissue and muscles. The muscles that hold it in place are also what allows the eye to have movement. The eye uses two nerves, oculomotor and optic. The oculomotor nerve is use to constrict the pupil and accomodation of the lens. The optic nerve is used for vision.

The eyeball is able to keep its shape due to pressure of fluid within two chambers. The anerior is filled with aqueous humor, this fluid may be replenished an injury occurs. However vitreous humor is a jellylike substance that is unable to be replenished and blindness may result from an injury. The exterior white part of the eye is called sclera.

The eye is covered by a delicate membrane known as conjunctiva, this membrane must be kept moist and this is done by the lacrimal glands (tear glands). Another important structure in the eye is the pupil. This structure acts like a camera lense and dilates and constricts to regulate light coming into the eye. Pupils should be equal but a condition known as anisocoria may cause pupils to be unequal. If pupils are unequal and the patient does not have this condition and the patient is unresponsive then a head injury should be suspected.

The Ear

The ear is a complex organ that is associated with hearing and balance. The ear is divided into three parts. The external ear is composed of the pinna, or auricle, which is the part lying outside of the head, and the external auditory canal, which leads in toward the tympanic membrane, or eardrum. The middle ear contains three small bones (the malleus, incus, and stapes) that move in response to sound waves hitting the tympanic membrane. It is connected to the nasal cavity by the eustachian tube, which permits equalization of pressure in the middle ear when external atmospheric pressure changes. The inner ear is composed of bony chambers filled with fluid. As the head moves, so does the fluid. In response, fine nerve endings within the fluid send impulses to the brain indicating the position of the head and the rate of change of position.

Pathophysiology

Facial Fractures

If a patient presents with facial fractures then it is within reason to suspect that the patient may have a closed head injury or cervical spine fractures. Some signs that you may note are:

  • Ecchymosis (discoloration of the skin)
  • Swelling
  • Pain on palpation
  • Crepitus (bones moving against each other)
  • Misalignment of the teeth
  • Facial deformities or asymmetry
  • Instability of the facial bones
  • Impaired ocular movement
  • Visual disturbances

Nasal Fractures

These injuries are usually accompanied by a nosebleed (epistaxis). Some signs will swelling, tenderness, and crepitus

Mandibular Fractures

Since significant force is required to fracture the mandible then it is possible that there is fractures in more than one place. Some signs of this are malocclusion (misalignment of the teeth), numbness of the chin, and inability to open the mouth. Take note if teeth are missing, patient experiences pain when opening their mouth to talk, range of motion that the jaw has. In very rare cases patients can experience temporomandibular joint dislocation. This is mostly commonly seen when someone has an exaggerated yawn which locks the mouth into a wide-open position

Maxillary Fractures

These types of fractures are mostly caused from massive trauma such as MVCs, falls, and assaults. These fractures are broken into three categories known as Le Fort fractures

  • Le Fort I fracture
    • Horizontal fracture of the maxilla that separates the hard palate and lower maxilla from the remainder of the skull
  • Le Fort II fracture
    • Fracture involving nasal bone and inferior maxilla. This fracture separates the nasal bone and lower maxilla from the facial skull and remainder of the cranial bones
  • Le Fort III fracture (craniofacial disjunction)
    • Fracture of all midfacial bones, separating the entire midface from the cranium

Orbital Fractures

Patients with tghese injuries may experience double vision (diplopia), some lose of sensation due to nerve damage may present.
