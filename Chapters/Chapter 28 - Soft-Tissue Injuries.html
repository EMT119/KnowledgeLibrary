Chapter 28 - Soft-Tissue Injuries

Soft-tissue injuries can be caused from several things ranging from blunt injuries to lacerations. These injuries are faily common and can cause tissue death due to hemorrhage or infection.

Anatomy and Physiology Review

Skin Structure and Function

The skin is the largest external organ and varies in thickness depending on location on the body and age of the individual. Skin is thicker on the soles of the feet where it is thinner on the eyelids

Skin is divided into two layers: epidermis and dermis

The epidermis is the external layer that is watertight and tough. This layer of skin is constantly replaced as cells are pushed to the surface from the germinal layers

The dermis is the inner layer of skin. The dermis contains many structures such as sweat glands, hair follicles, and sebaceous glands.

The dermis is vascular and small blood vessels reach up to the germinal cells, but no vessels are in the epidermis. However there are specialized nerve endings within the dermis

There are several orifices in the body that include the mouth, nose, anus, and vagina that are lined with mucous membranes. These surfaces are usually moist as they secrete a lubricant. The skin however plays a different role in regulating the body as it helps protect the body from foreign bodies as well as regulate temperature. When the body becomes how the vessels dilate and allows heat to radiate from the surface of the body. However when it is cold the vessels will constrict and shunt blood from the skin to prevent heat radiation.

Skin Tension lines

The skin is arranged in a way the provides tension all over the body. These patterns are known as tension lines Whenever a laceration occurs if it is parallel to the skin tension then it may remain closed, however if it is perpendicular to the tension then the laceration may require sutures or staples to remain closed.

Dynamic tension is in areas where muscles are present. This type of tension is varied based on the contraction of the muscle it is lying ontop of.

Pathophysiology of Closed and Open Injuries

There are three types of soft-tissue injuries

  • Closed injury
    • Damage occurs beneath skin or mucous membrane but the surface remains intact
  • Open injury
    • There is a break in the skin that exposes deeper tissues to potential contamination
  • Burns
    • When soft tissue receives more energy than it can absorb without injury

Closed Injuries

Soft-tissue injuries are characterized by a history of blunt trauma, pain at the injury site, and swelling beneath the skin.

  • Contustion
    • Bleeding beneath the skin that does not break the skin. Usually is from blunt force impacts. If the injury is great then fluid leaking into the damaged area will cause pain and swelling
  • Ecchymosis
    • A blue or black discoloration due to blood buildup
  • Hematoma
    • A pool of blood that has collected within damaged tissue or in a body cavity. This can be caused by any injury such as blunt force trauma or a fracture
    • Some can contain more than 1 liter of blood

In some injuries if an object is restricting or causes compression then a patient will had damage to the part of the body that is unable to receive blood flow. This is known as crush syndrome, this syndrome is when tissue becomes necrotic after blood that is trapped starts to release harmful products. Once the object that is trapping the blood is released the harmful products will flow the blood back to the heart, in most of these cases it will cause the patient to go into cardiac arrest (this is due to the high amounts of potassium). These patients may also have issues with renal failure due to the increased work on the kidneys.

Another syndrome that can cut off blood flow is compartment syndrome. This is when blood vessels become compressed due to pressure from fluids that are leaking around them.

Open Injuries

Open injuries bring some other issues than closed injuries. In open injuries there is there can be extensive bleeding, possibility of infection due to the protective layer being broken.

  • Abrasion
    • A superficial wound that is caused by friction when the body part rubs or scrapes across a rough or hard surface
    • Generally not severe, but can be if the wound covers a large surface area like in cases of road rash
  • Laceration
    • A cut caused by a sharp object or blunt force. These cuts can be smooth or jagged. If the laceration includes an artery or veins then there will be extensive bleeding
  • Avulsion
    • This injury is best defined as a flap. This is when an injury separates tissues so that it is hanging by a piece of the tissue.
  • Amputation

  • Penetrating wound
    • When a sharp object or a blunt object traveling at high speed penetrates the skin
  • Evisceration
    • When organs protrude from an injury

Pathyophysiology of Wound Healing

Infection

Whenever there is an open injury it poses the possibility of infection. Two of the most common types of bacterial infections are Staphylococcus and Streptococcus. It may take several days for signs of an infection to present, but some of these signs can include: erythema, pus, warmth, edema, and local discomfort.

If the patient develops red streaks next to their wound then this is most likely lymphangitis which is inflammation of the lymph channels.

Gangrene

This is when tissue starts to die due to the lack of blood floow to the tissue. Clostridium perfingens is an example of a toxin-producing bacterium that can develop into gangrene, it also produces a foul-smelling gas. Remember if it is not treated then it can progress into sepsis

Tetanus

This is caused by the Clostridium tetani bacterium, it generally causes painful muscle contractions that can fracture bones

Nectrotizing Fasciitis

This is also known as "the flesh-eating disease". It is due to inflammation of the fascia

Emergency Medical Care of Closed and Open Injuries

Closed Injuries

If the injury is minor then it can be managed by using the mnemonic RICES

RICES
Rest Keep the patient as comfortable as possible
Ice Apply cold packs to the injuried area (this can help with bleeding as well as it constricts the blood vessels)
Compression This helps stopn bleeding as it compresses the blood vessels
Elevate Raise the injury above the heart to help reduce swelling
Splint Helps prevent motion and further damage to the area
Remember to check Pulse, Motor, and Sensory before and after splinting

Management of Specific Wounds

Avulsion

If possible position the flap back to its original position and bandage

Amputation

Depending on the type of amputation it may be difficult to control bleeding. If the amputation is to a large area then it may be necessary to apply a tourniquet and treat the patient for hypovolemic shock.

Remember most of the time amputations can be reattached so it is important to keep the amputation sterile and bring it with you to the hospital.

Abdominal Wounds

If organs are protruding from the wound do NOT try to push them back in. Cover them with a sterile and moist gauze. Keep in mind that the patient will lose heat rapidly so it is important to keep them warm

Burns

When skin is burned it creates a leathery substance known as eschar. This can create issues since it does not act like normal skin and will not stretch when excess pressure is applied. As edema builds up it can cause compression on any structure underneath the eschar resulting in compartment syndrome.

Hypovolemic Shock

This type of shock can occur for two reasons: fluid loss and a volume shift of fluid. In burns it is generally a volume shift as fluid will leak out into the interstitial space. As this happens blood pressure will decrease causing the body to become tachycardic and vasoconstrict. These patients will require adequate fluid resuscitation

Burn Zones

There are three burn zones, these zones radiate from the greatest damage out

  • Zone of coagulation
    • Decreased blood flow and inflammation
  • Zone of stasis
    • May undergo tissue necrosis if perfusion is compromised
  • Zone of hyperemia
    • Least affected zone, typically recover in 7 to 10 days

Burn Depth

Like burn zones, burn depth is classified into three categories: superficial, partial thickness, and full thickness

  • Superficial (formerly first degree)
    • Skin turns red but does not blister
    • Quite painful
  • Partial-thickness burns (formerly second degree)
    • Involves the epidermis and portion of the dermis
    • Usually moist, mottled, and white to red
    • Blisters are common
    • Cause intense pain
  • Full-thickness (formerly third degree)
    • Extends through all skin layers
    • Can appear to be white, dark brown, or charred
    • If nerve endings have been destroyer then there will be no pain, however areas surrounding burn will be extremely painful

There are technically 6 categories to burns but in the prehospital setting the first three categories are the most often used

Burn Severity

There are five factors that can help you determine the severity of a burn

  1. Depth of the burn?
  2. Extent of the burn?
  3. Critical areas involved? or circumferential burns?
  4. Preexisting medical conditions that can cause complications
  5. Patient younger than 5 years or older than 55 years?
Classification of Burn Severity
Burn Classifications Criteria
Major Burns - Burns involving the hands, feet, face, major joints, or genitalia, or circumferential burns of other areas
- Full-thickness burns covering more than 10% of the TBSA
- Partial-thickness burns covering more than:
- 25% of the TBSA if age 10-50 years
- 20% of the TBSA if age younger than 10 years or older than 50 years
- Burns associated with respiratory injury (smoke or inhalation injury)
- Burns complicated by fractures or trauma
- High-voltage electrical burns
- Chemical burns
- Burns on patients younger than 5 years or older than 55 years that would be classified as moderate on young adults
Moderate burns - Full-thickness burns involving 2% to 10% of the TBSA (excluding hands, feet, face, genitalia, and upper airway)
- Partial-thickness burns covering:
- 15% - 25% of the TBSA if age 10 to 50 years
- 10% - 20% of the TBSA if age younger than 10 years or older than 50 years
- Superficial burns covering more than 50% of the TBSA
- Low-voltage electrical burns
- Major burn characteristics absent
Minor burns - Full-thickness burns covering less than 2% of the body's TBSA
- Partial-thickness burns covering:
- Less than 15% of the TBSA if age 10 to 15 years
- Less than 10% of the TBSA if age younger than 10 years or older than 50 years
- Superficial burns covering less than 50% of the TBSA
- Major burn characterisitcs absent

Burn Extent

The easiest way to calculate TBSA burned is by using the rule of nines, this method divdes the body into 11 sections with each representing roughly 9% of the TBSA

If the burn area is less than 10% then it is recommended to use the rule of palms which utilizes the patient's palm to represent roughly 1% of the TBSA

Emergency Medical Care of Burns

For patients with major burns then fluid resuscitation will be critical point in their possibility of survival. In these patients a special formula known as the consensus formula (formerly parkland formula).

2-4 mL x Patient's weight (kg) x Percentage of body surface area burned =
Total fluid in 24 hours

This formula also says to give half the fluid during the first 8 hours then give the remaining half over the next 16 hours. However if the patient shows signs of inadequate perfusion then it is permitted to do a 20mL/kg boluses to maintain perfusion

A important thing to note is to not fluid overload these patients in the field as the patient could develop pulmonary edeam or actue respiratory distress syndrome
