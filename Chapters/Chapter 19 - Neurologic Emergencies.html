Chapter 19 - Neurologic Emergencies

Anatomy and Physiology Review

The nervous system is divided into two parts. The central nervous system (CNS) and the peripheral nervous system (PNS)

The brain is divded into three parts: the brainstem, cerebellum, and cerebrum. The brainstem controls breathing, blood pressure, swallowing, and pupil constriction. The cerebellum controls muscle and body coordination, it coordinates voluntary movement of muscles.

The cerebrum is subdivided into two parts, the left and right hemispheres. Each hemisphere controls the opposite side of the body. However the cerebrum activity is distributed from front to back. The front controls emotion and thought, middle controls touch and movement, and the back processed sight.

There are twelve cranial nerve that run from the brain to the various parts of the head, ones that go to the rest of the body join in the spinal cord and exit through the foramen magnum.

Neurons and Impulse Transmission

The neuron is a fundamental element of the nervous system. Neurons are comprosied of three parts: cell body, axon, and dendrites. The axon is a projection from the cell body that extends toward another cell carrying signal away from the neruon. The axon could be wrapped in a sheat of myelin, this insulating substance that allows the cell to transmit a signal efficiently and consistently. Myelin also increases the speed of conduction.

Synapses

Synapses are the junctions where neurons interact. It is important to remember nerve cells do not actually have direct contact, but there is actually a slight gap. This gap allows for fine control. The communication occurs through chemical release called neurotransmitters from the first cell.

Neurotransmitters

Some examples of neurotransmitters are dopamine, acetylcholine, epinephrine and serotonin. These chemicals allow the electrical signal to be conducted from one nerve cell to the next.

Nerve cells have to respond or not, there is not such thing as a neuron firing weakly.

Pathophysiology

Stroke

Stroke otherwise known as a (cerebrovascular accident (CVA) is when there is an interruption of blood flow to the brain, this usually results in loss of function in the affected part of the brain. Due to the lack of blood flow to parts of the brain the cells will begin to die. These dead cells are called infarcted cells. After a cell dies there is little to do, however cell death can take several hours and can be prvented if care is rendered allowing for blood flow to be restored. If blood is obstructed but not fully blocked then this will allow cells to receieve the blood flow they need to survive, these cells are called ischemic cells. In these cases the cells receieve the blood flow they need to stay alive but not they will still be unable to function properly.

Interruption of blood flow can be caused by a few things, such as a thrombus which is a clot that has developed. A arterial rupture, or a cerebral embolism.

Keep in mind that a thrombus is a clot that develops locally while a embolism is a clot that formed somewhere else and traveled to the location.

There are two types of strokes, hemorrhagic and ischemic.

Ischemic Stroke

This type of stroke occurs when a blood vessel is blocked. Eventually if blood flow is not restored these cells will die.

Another disorder than can cause a obstruction is atherosclerosis. This disease causes a buildup of calcium and cholestrol inside the walls of the blood vessels. Overtime the plaque that is formed can cause a complete obstruction

When cells start to become ischemic they will switch to anaerobic metabolism. This type of metabolism is not made for long periods of time as it creates less amount of energy and it produces an acidic by-product.

One problem that can occur is if there is a partial obstruction in a carotid artery then there is a risk of smaller pieces of the clot breaking off and being carried deep into the brain.

Hemorrahic Stroke

This type of stroke occurs when there is bleeding within the brain. This can be caused by a blood vessel rupturing and the blood forming a clot. Hemorrahgic strokes can vary widely as it depends on where the bleeding is located inside the brain and the sze of the rupture.

As bleeding continues intracranial pressure (ICP) will begin to increase causes brain tissue to become compressed. Compression of the brain tissue causes oxygenated blood to be unable to reach those parts of the brain.

One reason for hemorrhagic strokes are aneurysms. Aneurysms can occur for some of the following reasons:

  • A small tear or defect occurs within the wall of an artery
  • Blood penetrates between the layers of the artery
  • Pressure builds up, and the initial small tear increases in size

When this occurs the wall of the artery is weakened causing it to be unable to withstand the pressure of blood flowing. As blood flows through the artery this weakened section will begin to bulge, this causes it to leak or fail catastrophically.

When an aneurysm ruptures and causes bleeding in the area between the covering of the brain this is called a subarachnoid hemorrhage. As bleeding continues and ICP increases the brain can be compressed and forced out through the foramen magnum, this process is called herniation

Intracranial Pressure

There are three substances within the skull that exert pressure, the brain, blood, and cerebrospinal fluid. It is imporant for this balance to be maintained as it allows the brain to fit snugly within the skull. If this wasn't the case there would be void areas within the skull, this would allow the brain to move around and potentially slam into the skull.

As ICP increases the amount of blood avaliable to the brain decreases.This pressure is known as cerebral perfusion pressue (CPP).

Multiple problems can arrise from increase ICP, one being the brain becoming ischemic due to lack of a blood supply or herniation.

Transient Ischemic Attack

In some patients the bodies normal processes will be able to destory a clot that is causing an obstruction. When this happens blood flow is restored to the brain and patients symptoms will resolve. If their symptoms resolve within 24 hours then these cases are known as transient ischemic attack (TIA).

The postivie part of TIAs is they leave no lasting damage or symptoms. However TIAs can be good warning signs fo a serious vascular condition. Approximately 1/3 of patients who experience a TIA will have an stroke in the future.

Signs and Symptoms of Stroke

  • Facial drooping
  • Sudden weakness or numbness in the face, arm, leg, or one side of the body
  • Decreased or absent movement and sensation on one side of the body
  • Ataxia (lack of muscle coordination) or loss of balance
  • Sudden vision loss in one eye; blurred or double vision (diplopia)
  • Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing)
  • Decreased level of responsiveness
  • Aphasia; difficulty expressing thoughts or inability to use the right words (expressive aphasia) or difficulty understanding spoke words (receptive aphasia)
  • Slurred speech (dysarthria)
  • Sudden and severe headache
  • Confusion
  • Dizziness
  • Weakness
  • Combativeness
  • Restlessness
  • Tongue deviation
  • Coma

Left Hemisphere Probelms

If this side of the brain has been affected the patient may present with aphasia which is the inability to produce or understand speech.

Right Hemisphere Problems

If the right side of the brain has been affected the patient will be unable to move mucles on the left side of the body.

Bleeding in the Brain

These patients may present with hypertension. It is important to remember hypertension can be the cause of the bleeding, but it can also be in response to the bleeding.

Conditions That May Mimic Stroke

  • Hypoglycemia
  • Postictal state
  • Subdural or epidural bleeding

The dura is a leathery covering over the brain next to the skull. A fracture can cause bleeding from an artery on top of the dura which results in pressure on the brain. This type of pressure usually occurs rapidly. On the other hand If the fracture causes bleeding below the dura it is usually from veins, these symptmos occur more slowly

Seizures

Seizures are described as sudden, erratic firings of neurons. Seizure patients can experience a variety of symptoms, they can range from a metallic tase in the mouth or involve the whole body with a complete loss of consciousness.

Tonic-Clonic Seizures

Tonic-clonic seizures (formerly grand mal seizures) usually follow a pattern, although not every patient will experience every step. The steps are as follows:
  • Aura is the sensation the patient experiences before a seizure occurs. Some examples of aura are:
    • Muscle twitching
    • Odd taste
    • Seeing lights
    • Hearing high-pitched noise
  • Loss of consciousness
  • Tonic phase (body-wide rigidity)
  • Hypertonic phase (arched back and rigidity)
  • Clonic phase are rhythmic contractions of major muscle groups
  • Postseizure is when the major muscle relax, nystagmus (rhythmic eye movement) can still occur
  • Postictal phase is the reset period of the brain, during this time patient will slowly return to preseizure

During tonic-clonic seizures the patients respirations can become erratic, loud, and patient may become apneic. These periods of apnea should be short and last no longer than 30 seconds.

Pseudoseizures

The main difference between tonic-clonic and pseudoseizures is that psuedoseizures are psychiatric in orgin. An imporant thing to remember is the patient is not intentionally causing this behavior.

Pseudoseizures are usually triggered from an emotional event, stress, lights, or pain.

Absence Seizures

These types of seizures present with little or no movement. Typically children will present with these types of seizures. They will usually stop moving, speaking, or freeze with a toy in their hand.

Partial Seizures

These types of seizures can be classified as simple partial or complex partial. These types of seizures involve a portion of the brain. These can be localized to just one spot or move in a wakelike manner.

Simple partial seizures involve one part of the body, this can be from movement or altered sensations. This movement can either stay in one part of the body or spread in a wave-like movement.

Complex partial seizures involve subtle changes in the LOC. Patients may become confused or less alert, experience hallucinations or be unable to speak.

Status Epilepticus

This seizure lasts longer for more than 4 or 5 minutes, these seizures also can occur consecutively without return of consciousness. This time frame does vary and is best to check your local protocol.

The Postictal State

After a seizure has occured the patient's muscles relax, become flacid or floppy, and breathing becomes laborered. The increased respiratory rate helps to compensate for the buildup of acids. After the pH in the body has balanced the breathing will return to normal.

In some cases there can be hemiparesis (weakness on side of the body). The postictal state causes the patient to be lethargic and confused, this can cause issues as the patient may become combative.

Altered Mental Status

This is the most common type of neurologic emergency. Altered mental status means the patient is not thinking clearly or incapable of being aroused. Causes for this can vary such as: hypoglycemia, hypoxemia, intoxication, drug overdose, unrecognized head injury, brain infection, body temperature abnormalities, brain tumors, and overdoses.

Hypoglycemia

Since glucose is needed for the brain to function patients with hypoglycemia have the possibility to present with stroke like symptoms. The difference is that a patient having a stroke will attempt to communicate normally where a patient with hypoglycemia almost always has an altered mental status.

Syncope

Syncope (fainting) is a sudden and temporary loss of consciousness. This medical condition affects 1% to 3% of all emergency department visits. When someone has a syncopal episode it is important to determine what caused this episode to happen, some reasons are: cardiac dysrhythmia, myocardial infarction, dehydration, hypoglycemia, and a vasovagal episode.

Headache

Tension headaches, migraines, and sinus headaches are the most common types of headaches and are condiered non-life threatening.

A migrain is a complex condition that is caused by minor instability within certain clusters of neurons and also changes in blood vessel size at the base of the brain. Patients may experience an aura, and unilateral, focused pain that spreads over time. Patients will describe the pain as throbbing, pounding, or pulsating. Migraines can last over several days.

There are two other type of headaches, cluter headaches and sinus headaches. Cluster headaches last for 30 to 45 minutes, and may occur multiple times a day. Sinus headaches however are caused by inflammation or infection within the sinus cavities of the fact. This pain is located in the superior portions of the face and increases with bending the head forward.

Emergency Medical Care

Hypoglycemia

There are two medications that can be used to treat hypoglycemia, these are dextrose and glucagon. If patient is hypotensive administer 20mL/kg of normal fluid. Be careful when administering a fluid bolus as giving patients excess fluid in hemorrhagic strokes can increase ICP.

It is recommended to adminster 12.5g of dextrose if there is no blood glucose analysis monitor avaliable. When adminstering dextrose it is preferrable to obtain an 18 gauge IV as D50 is very viscous. MAKE SURE IV IS PATENT BEFORE ADMINSTERING DEXTROSE, if D50 makes it into the interstitial space it can cause severe damage to muscles, nerves, and skin. The usualy dosage of D50 is 25g or one full syringe. The onset is 30 seconds to 2 minutes.

If you are unable to obtain vascular access administer 0.5 to 1mg of glucagon intramuscularly. LOC and blood glucose analysis should increase within 20 minutes. Glucagon can be repeated up to a maximum of three doses.

Another option to help raise blood glucose is oral glucose. These usually come in 25g (one tube). However this option is only viable if the patient is able to maintain their own airway. Some alternatives are:

  • Cake icing
  • Plain chocolate bar
  • Orange juice with suagr added

There is currently no way to lower blood sugar in the prehospital setting.

Stroke/TIA, Syncope, and Headache

Perform supportive care. Maintain an SPO2 above 94%, support airway and breathing.
