Pathophysiology

Acute abdomen is a medical term that is used to refer to the sudden onset of abdominal pain. This usually indicates the irriation of the peritoneum, the thin membrane that lines the abdominal cavity. This condition is known as peritonitis. This condition can be caused by infection, penetrating abdominal injuries, blunt injuries that are severe enough to damage abdominal organs. Clinical signs for this are abdominal tenderness and distention.

It is important to remember that urologic pain is usually referred pain. This means that pain from an organ may be interpretted as pain in the shoulder. This is caused from the two sepearate nervous systems coming together.

During normal digestion the intestine move in a wave like pattern known as peristalsis. However when paralysis (ileus) will cause the stomach be unable to empty into the intestine. This causes gas and feces to build up and cause abdominal distention. If this happens the only way the stomach is able to empty itself is by vomiting (emesis). This is why nausea and vomiting is usually associated with peritonitis.

Soemtimes peritonitis can be caused by loss of body fluid into the abdominal cavity. This shift of fluid decreases the circulating blood volume. Overtime this can cause hypovolemic shock.

Patient's with diverticulitis, cholecysitis, or appendicitis may or may not have fever. Appendicitis usually will not present with a fever until the appendix ruptures.

Causes of Acute Abdomen

The abdominal cavity normally contains small amounts of peritoneal fluid that lubricates the organs. Peritonitis can be caused by any foreign material that builds up inside cavity. Some of these fluids could be pus, blood, feces, urine, gastric juice, intestinal contents, bile, pancreatic juice, amniotic fluid, or other materials

GI hemorrhages can occur in the upper and lower parts of the GI tract. If bleeding occurs in the esophagus it could be caused from esophagitis, esophageal varices, or Mallory-Weiss tear (reulting from excessive vomiting). Hematemesis (vomiting blood) is usually seen in upper GI bleeds. Blood will usually appear to be bright read, or have the appearance of coffe grounds.

Bleeding in the lower GI tract can be caused from bowel inflammation, diverticulosis, diverticulitis, cancer, and hemorrhoids. melena (dark tarry stools) will be present from the digestion of blodo.
